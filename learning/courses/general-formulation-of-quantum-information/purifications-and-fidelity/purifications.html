Definition of purifications​

Let us begin with a precise mathematical definition for purifications.

Definition

Suppose X\mathsf{X}X is a system in a state represented by a density matrix ρ,\rho,ρ, and ∣ψ⟩\vert\psi\rangle∣ψ⟩ is a quantum state vector of a pair (X,Y)(\mathsf{X},\mathsf{Y})(X,Y) that leaves ρ\rhoρ when Y\mathsf{Y}Y is traced out:

ρ=Tr⁡Y(∣ψ⟩⟨ψ∣).\rho = \operatorname{Tr}_{\mathsf{Y}} \bigl( \vert \psi\rangle\langle\psi\vert\bigr).ρ=TrY​(∣ψ⟩⟨ψ∣).

The state vector ∣ψ⟩\vert\psi\rangle∣ψ⟩ is then said to be a purification of ρ.\rho.ρ.

The pure state ∣ψ⟩⟨ψ∣,\vert\psi\rangle\langle\psi\vert,∣ψ⟩⟨ψ∣, expressed as a density matrix rather than a quantum state vector, is also commonly referred to as a purification of ρ\rhoρ when the equation in the definition is true, but we'll generally use the term to refer to a quantum state vector.

The term purification is also used more generally when the ordering of the systems is reversed, when the names of the systems and states are different (of course), and when there are more than two systems. For instance, if ∣ψ⟩\vert \psi \rangle∣ψ⟩ is a quantum state vector representing a pure state of a compound system (A,B,C),(\mathsf{A},\mathsf{B},\mathsf{C}),(A,B,C), and the equation

is true for a density matrix ρ\rhoρ representing a state of the system (A,C),(\mathsf{A},\mathsf{C}),(A,C), then ∣ψ⟩\vert\psi\rangle∣ψ⟩ is still referred to as a purification of ρ.\rho.ρ.

For the purposes of this lesson, however, we'll focus on the specific form described in the definition. Properties and facts concerning purifications, according to this definition, can typically be generalized to more than two systems by re-ordering and partitioning the systems into two compound systems, one playing the role of X\mathsf{X}X and the other playing the role of Y.\mathsf{Y}.Y.

Existence of purifications​

Suppose that X\mathsf{X}X and Y\mathsf{Y}Y are any two systems and ρ\rhoρ is a given state of X.\mathsf{X}.X. We will prove that there exists a quantum state vector ∣ψ⟩\vert\psi\rangle∣ψ⟩ of (X,Y)(\mathsf{X},\mathsf{Y})(X,Y) that purifies ρ\rhoρ — which is another way of saying that ∣ψ⟩\vert\psi\rangle∣ψ⟩ is a purification of ρ\rhoρ — provided that the system Y\mathsf{Y}Y is large enough. Fewer classical states of Y\mathsf{Y}Y are required for some states ρ;\rho;ρ; in general, rank⁡(ρ)\operatorname{rank}(\rho)rank(ρ) classical states of Y\mathsf{Y}Y are necessary and sufficient for the existence of a quantum state vector of (X,Y)(\mathsf{X},\mathsf{Y})(X,Y) that purifies ρ.\rho.ρ.

Schmidt decompositions​

Next, we will discuss Schmidt decompositions, which are expressions of quantum state vectors of pairs of systems that take a certain form. Schmidt decompositions are closely connected with purifications, and they're very useful in their own right. Indeed, when reasoning about a given quantum state vector ∣ψ⟩\vert\psi\rangle∣ψ⟩ of a pair of systems, the first step is often to identify or consider a Schmidt decomposition of this state.

∣ψ⟩=∑a=0r−1pa ∣xa⟩⊗∣ya⟩,\vert \psi\rangle = \sum_{a = 0}^{r-1} \sqrt{p_a}\, \vert x_a\rangle \otimes \vert y_a \rangle,∣ψ⟩=a=0∑r−1​pa​​∣xa​⟩⊗∣ya​⟩,

where p0,…,pr−1p_0,\ldots,p_{r-1}p0​,…,pr−1​ are positive real numbers summing to 111 and both of the sets {∣x0⟩,…,∣xr−1⟩}\{\vert x_0\rangle,\ldots,\vert x_{r-1}\rangle\}{∣x0​⟩,…,∣xr−1​⟩} and {∣y0⟩,…,∣yr−1⟩}\{\vert y_0\rangle,\ldots,\vert y_{r-1}\rangle\}{∣y0​⟩,…,∣yr−1​⟩} are orthonormal.

{∣x0⟩,…,∣xr−1⟩}and{∣y0⟩,…,∣yr−1⟩},\{\vert x_0\rangle,\ldots,\vert x_{r-1}\rangle\} \quad\text{and}\quad \{\vert y_0\rangle,\ldots,\vert y_{r-1}\rangle\},{∣x0​⟩,…,∣xr−1​⟩}and{∣y0​⟩,…,∣yr−1​⟩},

Superdense coding​

In the superdense coding protocol, Alice and Bob share an e-bit, meaning that Alice holds a qubit A,\mathsf{A},A, Bob holds a qubit B,\mathsf{B},B, and together the pair (A,B)(\mathsf{A},\mathsf{B})(A,B) is in the ∣ϕ+⟩\vert\phi^{+}\rangle∣ϕ+⟩ Bell state. The protocol describes how Alice can transform this shared state into any one of the four Bell states, ∣ϕ+⟩,\vert\phi^+\rangle,∣ϕ+⟩, ∣ϕ−⟩,\vert\phi^-\rangle,∣ϕ−⟩, ∣ψ+⟩,\vert\psi^+\rangle,∣ψ+⟩, and ∣ψ−⟩,\vert\psi^-\rangle,∣ψ−⟩, by applying a unitary operation to her qubit A.\mathsf{A}.A. Once she has done this, she sends A\mathsf{A}A to Bob, and then Bob performs a measurement on the pair (A,B)(\mathsf{A},\mathsf{B})(A,B) to see which Bell state he holds.

For all four Bell states, the reduced state of Bob's qubit B\mathsf{B}B is the completely mixed state.

Tr⁡A(∣ϕ+⟩⟨ϕ+∣)=Tr⁡A(∣ϕ−⟩⟨ϕ−∣)=Tr⁡A(∣ψ+⟩⟨ψ+∣)=Tr⁡A(∣ψ−⟩⟨ψ−∣)=I2\operatorname{Tr}_{\mathsf{A}}(\vert\phi^+\rangle\langle\phi^+\vert) = \operatorname{Tr}_{\mathsf{A}}(\vert\phi^-\rangle\langle\phi^-\vert) = \operatorname{Tr}_{\mathsf{A}}(\vert\psi^+\rangle\langle\psi^+\vert) = \operatorname{Tr}_{\mathsf{A}}(\vert\psi^-\rangle\langle\psi^-\vert) = \frac{\mathbb{I}}{2}TrA​(∣ϕ+⟩⟨ϕ+∣)=TrA​(∣ϕ−⟩⟨ϕ−∣)=TrA​(∣ψ+⟩⟨ψ+∣)=TrA​(∣ψ−⟩⟨ψ−∣)=2I​

By the unitary equivalence of purifications, we immediately conclude that for each Bell state there must exist a unitary operation on Alice's qubit A\mathsf{A}A alone that transforms ∣ϕ+⟩\vert\phi^+\rangle∣ϕ+⟩ into the chosen Bell state. Although this does not reveal the precise details of the protocol, the unitary equivalence of purifications does immediately imply that superdense coding is possible.

We can also conclude that generalizations of superdense coding to larger systems are always possible, provided that we replace the Bell states with any orthonormal basis of purifications of the completely mixed state.

Cryptographic implications​

The unitary equivalence of purifications has implications concerning the implementation of cryptographic primitives using quantum information. For instance, the unitary equivalence of purifications reveals that it is impossible to implement an ideal form of bit commitment using quantum information.

The bit commitment primitive involves two participants, Alice and Bob (who don't trust one another), and has two phases.

  • The first phase is the commit phase, through which Alice commits to a binary value b∈{0,1}.b\in\{0,1\}.b∈{0,1}. This commitment must be binding, which means that Alice cannot change her mind, as well as concealing, which means that Bob can't tell which value Alice has committed to.
  • The second phase is the reveal phase, in which the bit committed by Alice becomes known to Bob, who should then be convinced that it was truly the committed value that was revealed.

In intuitive, operational terms, the first phase of bit commitment should function as if Alice writes a binary value on a piece of paper, locks the paper inside of a safe, and gives the safe to Bob while keeping the key for herself. Alice has committed to the binary value written on the paper because the safe is in Bob's possession (so it's binding), but because Bob can't open the safe he can't tell which value Alice committed to (so it's concealing). The second phase should work as if Alice hands the key to the safe to Bob, so that he can open the safe to reveal the value to which Alice committed.

As it turns out, it is impossible to implement a perfect bit commitment protocol by means of quantum information alone, for this contradicts the unitary equivalence of purifications. Here is a high-level summary of an argument that establishes this.

To begin, we can assume Alice and Bob only perform unitary operations or introduce new initialized systems as the protocol is executed. The fact that every channel has a Stinespring representation allows us to make this assumption.

At the end of the commit phase of the protocol, Bob holds in his possession some compound system that must be in one of two quantum states: ρ0\rho_0ρ0​ if Alice committed to the value 000 and ρ1\rho_1ρ1​ if Alice committed to the value 1.1.1. In order for the protocol to be perfectly concealing, Bob should not be able to tell the difference between these two states — so it must be that ρ0=ρ1.\rho_0 = \rho_1.ρ0​=ρ1​. (Otherwise there would be a measurement that discriminates these states probabilistically.)

However, because Alice and Bob have only used unitary operations, the state of all of the systems involved in the protocol together after the commit phase must be in a pure state. In particular, suppose that ∣ψ0⟩\vert\psi_0\rangle∣ψ0​⟩ is the pure state of all of the systems involved in the protocol when Alice commits to 0,0,0, and ∣ψ1⟩\vert\psi_1\rangle∣ψ1​⟩ is the pure state of all of the systems involved in the protocol when Alice commits to 1.1.1. If we write A\mathsf{A}A and B\mathsf{B}B to denote Alice and Bob's (possibly compound) systems, then

ρ0=Tr⁡A(∣ψ0⟩⟨ψ0∣)ρ1=Tr⁡A(∣ψ1⟩⟨ψ1∣).\begin{aligned} \rho_0 & = \operatorname{Tr}_{\mathsf{A}}(\vert\psi_0\rangle\langle\psi_0\vert)\\[1mm] \rho_1 & = \operatorname{Tr}_{\mathsf{A}}(\vert\psi_1\rangle\langle\psi_1\vert). \end{aligned}ρ0​ρ1​​=TrA​(∣ψ0​⟩⟨ψ0​∣)=TrA​(∣ψ1​⟩⟨ψ1​∣).​

Given the requirement that ρ0=ρ1\rho_0 = \rho_1ρ0​=ρ1​ for a perfectly concealing protocol, we find that ∣ψ0⟩\vert\psi_0\rangle∣ψ0​⟩ and ∣ψ1⟩\vert\psi_1\rangle∣ψ1​⟩ are purifications of the same state — and so, by the unitary equivalence of purifications, there must exist a unitary operation UUU on A\mathsf{A}A alone such that

(U⊗IB)∣ψ0⟩=∣ψ1⟩.(U\otimes\mathbb{I}_{\mathsf{B}})\vert\psi_0\rangle = \vert\psi_1\rangle.(U⊗IB​)∣ψ0​⟩=∣ψ1​⟩.

Alice is therefore free to change her commitment from 000 to 111 by applying UUU to A,\mathsf{A},A, or from 111 to 000 by applying U†,U^{\dagger},U†, and so the hypothetical protocol being considered completely fails to be binding.
