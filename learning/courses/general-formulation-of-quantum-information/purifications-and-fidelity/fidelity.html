Fidelity

Hindi pa naisalin

Ang pahinang ito ay hindi pa naisalin. Nakikita mo ang orihinal na bersyon sa Ingles.

In this part of the lesson, we'll discuss the fidelity between quantum states, which is a measure of their similarity — or how much they "overlap."

Given two quantum state vectors, the fidelity between the pure states associated with these quantum state vectors equals the absolute value of the inner product between the quantum state vectors. This provides a basic way to measure their similarity: the result is a value between 000 and 1,1,1, with larger values indicating greater similarity. In particular, the value is zero for orthogonal states (by definition), while the value is 111 for states equivalent up to a global phase.

Intuitively speaking, the fidelity can be seen as an extension of this basic measure of similarity, from quantum state vectors to density matrices.

Definition of fidelity​

It's fitting to begin with a definition of fidelity. At first glance, the definition that follows might look unusual or mysterious, and perhaps not easy to work with. The function it defines, however, turns out to have many interesting properties and multiple alternative formulations, making it much nicer to work with than it may initially appear.

Definition

Let ρ\rhoρ and σ\sigmaσ be density matrices representing quantum states of the same system. The fidelity between ρ\rhoρ and σ\sigmaσ is defined as

F⁡(ρ,σ)=Tr⁡ρσρ.\operatorname{F}(\rho,\sigma) = \operatorname{Tr}\sqrt{\sqrt{\rho} \sigma \sqrt{\rho}}.F(ρ,σ)=Trρ​σρ​​.
Remark

Although this is a common definition, it is also common that the fidelity is defined as the square of the quantity defined here, which is then referred to as the root-fidelity. Neither definition is right or wrong — it's essentially a matter of preference. Nevertheless, one must always be careful to understand or clarify which definition is being used.

To make sense of the formula in the definition, notice first that ρσρ\sqrt{\rho} \sigma \sqrt{\rho}ρ​σρ​ is a positive semidefinite matrix:

ρσρ=M†M\sqrt{\rho} \sigma \sqrt{\rho} = M^{\dagger} Mρ​σρ​=M†M

for M=σρ.M = \sqrt{\sigma}\sqrt{\rho}.M=σ​ρ​. Like all positive semidefinite matrices, this positive semidefinite matrix has a unique positive semidefinite square root, the trace of which is the fidelity.

For every square matrix M,M,M, the eigenvalues of the two positive semidefinite matrices M†MM^{\dagger} MM†M and MM†M M^{\dagger}MM† are always the same, and hence the same is true for the square roots of these matrices. Choosing M=σρM = \sqrt{\sigma}\sqrt{\rho}M=σ​ρ​ and using the fact that the trace of a square matrix is the sum of its eigenvalues, we find that

F⁡(ρ,σ)=Tr⁡ρσρ=Tr⁡M†M=Tr⁡MM†=Tr⁡σρσ=F⁡(σ,ρ).\begin{aligned} \operatorname{F}(\rho,\sigma) & = \operatorname{Tr}\sqrt{\sqrt{\rho} \sigma \sqrt{\rho}}\\ & = \operatorname{Tr}\sqrt{M^{\dagger} M} = \operatorname{Tr}\sqrt{M M^{\dagger}}\\ & = \operatorname{Tr}\sqrt{\sqrt{\sigma} \rho \sqrt{\sigma}}\\ & = \operatorname{F}(\sigma,\rho). \end{aligned}F(ρ,σ)​=Trρ​σρ​​=TrM†M​=TrMM†​=Trσ​ρσ​​=F(σ,ρ).​

So, although it is not immediate from the definition, the fidelity is symmetric in its two arguments.

Fidelity in terms of the trace norm​

An equivalent way to express the fidelity is by this formula:

F⁡(ρ,σ)=∥σρ∥1.\operatorname{F}(\rho,\sigma) = \bigl\|\sqrt{\sigma}\sqrt{\rho}\bigr\|_1.F(ρ,σ)=​σ​ρ​​1​.

Here we see the trace norm, which we encountered in the previous lesson in the context of state discrimination. The trace norm of a (not necessarily square) matrix MMM can be defined as

∥M∥1=Tr⁡M†M,\| M \|_1 = \operatorname{Tr}\sqrt{M^{\dagger} M},∥M∥1​=TrM†M​,

and by applying this definition to the matrix σρ\sqrt{\sigma}\sqrt{\rho}σ​ρ​ we obtain the formula in the definition.

An alternative way to express the trace norm of a (square) matrix MMM is through this formula.

∥M∥1=max⁡U unitary∣Tr⁡(MU)∣.\| M \|_1 = \max_{U\:\text{unitary}} \bigl\vert \operatorname{Tr}(M U) \bigr\vert.∥M∥1​=Uunitarymax​​Tr(MU)​.

Here the maximum is over all unitary matrices UUU having the same number of rows and columns as M.M.M. Applying this formula in the situation at hand reveals another expression of the fidelity.

F⁡(ρ,σ)=max⁡U unitary∣Tr⁡(σρ U)∣\operatorname{F}(\rho,\sigma) = \max_{U\:\text{unitary}} \bigl\vert\operatorname{Tr}\bigl( \sqrt{\sigma}\sqrt{\rho}\, U\bigr) \bigr\vertF(ρ,σ)=Uunitarymax​​Tr(σ​ρ​U)​

Fidelity for pure states​

One last point on the definition of fidelity is that every pure state is (as a density matrix) equal to its own square root, which allows the formula for the fidelity to be simplified considerably when one or both of the states is pure. In particular, if one of the two states is pure we have the following formula.

F⁡(∣ϕ⟩⟨ϕ∣,σ)=⟨ϕ∣σ∣ϕ⟩\operatorname{F}\bigl( \vert\phi\rangle\langle\phi\vert, \sigma \bigr) = \sqrt{\langle \phi\vert \sigma \vert \phi \rangle}F(∣ϕ⟩⟨ϕ∣,σ)=⟨ϕ∣σ∣ϕ⟩​

If both states are pure, the formula simplifies to the absolute value of the inner product of the corresponding quantum state vectors, as was mentioned at the start of the section.

F⁡(∣ϕ⟩⟨ϕ∣,∣ψ⟩⟨ψ∣)=∣⟨ϕ∣ψ⟩∣\operatorname{F}\bigl( \vert\phi\rangle\langle\phi\vert, \vert\psi\rangle\langle\psi\vert \bigr) = \bigl\vert \langle \phi\vert \psi \rangle \bigr\vertF(∣ϕ⟩⟨ϕ∣,∣ψ⟩⟨ψ∣)=​⟨ϕ∣ψ⟩​

Basic properties of fidelity​

The fidelity has many remarkable properties and several alternative formulations. Here are just a few basic properties listed without proofs.

  1. For any two density matrices ρ\rhoρ and σ\sigmaσ having the same size, the fidelity F⁡(ρ,σ)\operatorname{F}(\rho,\sigma)F(ρ,σ) lies between zero and one: 0≤F⁡(ρ,σ)≤1.0\leq \operatorname{F}(\rho,\sigma) \leq 1.0≤F(ρ,σ)≤1. It is the case that F⁡(ρ,σ)=0\operatorname{F}(\rho,\sigma)=0F(ρ,σ)=0 if and only if ρ\rhoρ and σ\sigmaσ have orthogonal images (so they can be discriminated without error), and F⁡(ρ,σ)=1\operatorname{F}(\rho,\sigma)=1F(ρ,σ)=1 if and only if ρ=σ.\rho = \sigma.ρ=σ.
  2. The fidelity is multiplicative, meaning that the fidelity between two product states is equal to the product of the individual fidelities: F⁡(ρ1⊗⋯⊗ρm,σ1⊗⋯⊗σm)=F⁡(ρ1,σ1)⋯F⁡(ρm,σm).\operatorname{F}(\rho_1\otimes\cdots\otimes\rho_m,\sigma_1\otimes\cdots\otimes\sigma_m) = \operatorname{F}(\rho_1,\sigma_1)\cdots \operatorname{F}(\rho_m,\sigma_m).F(ρ1​⊗⋯⊗ρm​,σ1​⊗⋯⊗σm​)=F(ρ1​,σ1​)⋯F(ρm​,σm​).
  3. The fidelity between states is nondecreasing under the action of any channel. That is, if ρ\rhoρ and σ\sigmaσ are density matrices and Φ\PhiΦ is a channel that can take these two states as input, then it is necessarily the case that F⁡(ρ,σ)≤F⁡(Φ(ρ),Φ(σ)).\operatorname{F}(\rho,\sigma) \leq \operatorname{F}(\Phi(\rho),\Phi(\sigma)).F(ρ,σ)≤F(Φ(ρ),Φ(σ)).
  4. The Fuchs-van de Graaf inequalities establish a close (though not exact) relationship between fidelity and trace distance: for any two states ρ\rhoρ and σ\sigmaσ we have 1−12∥ρ−σ∥1≤F⁡(ρ,σ)≤1−14∥ρ−σ∥12.1 - \frac{1}{2}\|\rho - \sigma\|_1 \leq \operatorname{F}(\rho,\sigma) \leq \sqrt{1 - \frac{1}{4}\|\rho - \sigma\|_1^2}.1−21​∥ρ−σ∥1​≤F(ρ,σ)≤1−41​∥ρ−σ∥12​​.

The final property can be expressed in the form of a figure:

Specifically, for any choice of states ρ\rhoρ and σ\sigmaσ of the same system, the horizontal line that crosses the yyy-axis at F⁡(ρ,σ)\operatorname{F}(\rho,\sigma)F(ρ,σ) and the vertical line that crosses the xxx-axis at 12∥ρ−σ∥1\frac{1}{2}\|\rho-\sigma\|_121​∥ρ−σ∥1​ must intersect within the gray region bordered below by the line y=1−xy = 1-xy=1−x and above by the unit circle. The most interesting region of this figure from a practical viewpoint is the upper left-hand corner of the gray region: if the fidelity between two states is close to one, then their trace distance is close to zero, and vice versa.

Gentle measurement lemma​

Next we'll take a look at a simple but important fact, known as the gentle measurement lemma, which connects fidelity to non-destructive measurements. It's a very useful lemma that comes up from time to time, and it's also noteworthy because the seemingly clunky definition for the fidelity actually makes the lemma very easy to prove.

The set-up is as follows. Let X\mathsf{X}X be a system in a state ρ\rhoρ and let {P0,…,Pm−1}\{P_0,\ldots,P_{m-1}\}{P0​,…,Pm−1​} be a collection of positive semidefinite matrices representing a general measurement of X.\mathsf{X}.X. Suppose further that if this measurement is performed on the system X\mathsf{X}X while it's in the state ρ,\rho,ρ, one of the outcomes is highly likely. To be concrete, let's assume that the likely measurement outcome is 0,0,0, and specifically let's assume that

Tr⁡(P0ρ)>1−ε\operatorname{Tr}(P_0 \rho) > 1 - \varepsilonTr(P0​ρ)>1−ε

for a small positive real number ε>0.\varepsilon > 0.ε>0.

What the gentle measurement lemma states is that, under these assumptions, the non-destructive measurement obtained from {P0,…,Pm−1}\{P_0,\ldots,P_{m-1}\}{P0​,…,Pm−1​} through Naimark's theorem causes only a small disturbance to ρ\rhoρ in case the likely measurement outcome 000 is observed.

More specifically, the lemma states that the fidelity-squared between ρ\rhoρ and the state we obtain from the non-destructive measurement, conditioned on the outcome being 0,0,0, is greater than 1−ε.1-\varepsilon.1−ε.

F⁡(ρ,P0ρP0Tr⁡(P0ρ))2>1−ε.\operatorname{F}\Biggl(\rho,\frac{\sqrt{P_0}\rho\sqrt{P_0}}{\operatorname{Tr}(P_0\rho)}\Biggr)^2 > 1-\varepsilon.F(ρ,Tr(P0​ρ)P0​​ρP0​​​)2>1−ε.

We'll need a basic fact about measurements to prove this. The measurement matrices P0,…,Pm−1P_0, \ldots, P_{m-1}P0​,…,Pm−1​ are positive semidefinite and sum to the identity, which allows us to conclude that all of the eigenvalues of P0P_0P0​ are real numbers between 000 and 1.1.1. This follows from the fact that, for any unit vector ∣ψ⟩,\vert\psi\rangle,∣ψ⟩, the value ⟨ψ∣Pa∣ψ⟩\langle \psi \vert P_a \vert \psi \rangle⟨ψ∣Pa​∣ψ⟩ is a nonnegative real number for each a∈{0,…,m−1}a\in\{0,\ldots,m-1\}a∈{0,…,m−1} (because each PaP_aPa​ is positive semidefinite), together with these numbers summing to one.

∑a=0m−1⟨ψ∣Pa∣ψ⟩=⟨ψ∣(∑a=0m−1Pa)∣ψ⟩=⟨ψ∣I∣ψ⟩=1.\sum_{a = 0}^{m-1} \langle \psi \vert P_a \vert \psi \rangle = \langle \psi \vert \Biggl(\sum_{a = 0}^{m-1} P_a \Biggr) \vert \psi \rangle = \langle \psi \vert \mathbb{I} \vert \psi \rangle = 1.a=0∑m−1​⟨ψ∣Pa​∣ψ⟩=⟨ψ∣(a=0∑m−1​Pa​)∣ψ⟩=⟨ψ∣I∣ψ⟩=1.

Tr⁡(P0ρ)≥Tr⁡(P0ρ)\operatorname{Tr}\bigl( \sqrt{P_0} \rho\bigr) \geq \operatorname{Tr}\bigl( P_0 \rho\bigr)Tr(P0​​ρ)≥Tr(P0​ρ)

F⁡(ρ,P0ρP0Tr⁡(P0ρ))=Tr⁡ρP0ρP0ρTr⁡(P0ρ)=Tr⁡(ρP0ρTr⁡(P0ρ))2=Tr⁡(ρP0ρTr⁡(P0ρ))=Tr⁡(P0ρ)Tr⁡(P0ρ)\begin{aligned} \operatorname{F}\Biggl(\rho,\frac{\sqrt{P_0}\rho\sqrt{P_0}}{\operatorname{Tr}(P_0\rho)}\Biggr) & = \operatorname{Tr}\sqrt{\frac{\sqrt{\rho}\sqrt{P_0}\rho\sqrt{P_0}\sqrt{\rho}}{ \operatorname{Tr}(P_0\rho)}}\\ & = \operatorname{Tr}\sqrt{\Biggl(\frac{\sqrt{\rho}\sqrt{P_0}\sqrt{\rho}}{ \sqrt{\operatorname{Tr}(P_0\rho)}}\Biggr)^2}\\ & = \operatorname{Tr}\Biggl(\frac{\sqrt{\rho}\sqrt{P_0}\sqrt{\rho}}{ \sqrt{\operatorname{Tr}(P_0\rho)}}\Biggr)\\ & = \frac{\operatorname{Tr}\bigl(\sqrt{P_0}\rho\bigr)}{\sqrt{\operatorname{Tr}(P_0\rho)}} \end{aligned}F(ρ,Tr(P0​ρ)P0​​ρP0​​​)​=TrTr(P0​ρ)ρ​P0​​ρP0​​ρ​​​=Tr(Tr(P0​ρ)​ρ​P0​​ρ​​)2​=Tr(Tr(P0​ρ)​ρ​P0​​ρ​​)=Tr(P0​ρ)​Tr(P0​​ρ)​​

Uhlmann's theorem​

To conclude the lesson, we'll take a look at Uhlmann's theorem, which is a fundamental fact about the fidelity that connects it with the notion of a purification. What the theorem says, in simple terms, is that the fidelity between any two quantum states is equal to the maximum inner product (in absolute value) between two purifications of those states.

Theorem

Uhlmann's theorem: Let ρ\rhoρ and σ\sigmaσ be density matrices representing states of a system X,\mathsf{X},X, and let Y\mathsf{Y}Y be a system having at least as many classical states as X.\mathsf{X}.X. The fidelity between ρ\rhoρ and σ\sigmaσ is given by

F⁡(ρ,σ)=max⁡{∣⟨ϕ∣ψ⟩∣ : Tr⁡Y(∣ϕ⟩⟨ϕ∣)=ρ,  Tr⁡Y(∣ψ⟩⟨ψ∣)=σ}, \operatorname{F}(\rho,\sigma) = \max\bigl\{ \vert \langle \phi \vert \psi \rangle \vert \,:\, \operatorname{Tr}_{\mathsf{Y}}\bigl(\vert\phi\rangle\langle\phi\vert\bigr) = \rho,\; \operatorname{Tr}_{\mathsf{Y}}\bigl(\vert\psi\rangle\langle\psi\vert\bigr) = \sigma\bigr\},F(ρ,σ)=max{∣⟨ϕ∣ψ⟩∣:TrY​(∣ϕ⟩⟨ϕ∣)=ρ,TrY​(∣ψ⟩⟨ψ∣)=σ},

where the maximum is taken over all quantum state vectors ∣ϕ⟩\vert\phi\rangle∣ϕ⟩ and ∣ψ⟩\vert\psi\rangle∣ψ⟩ of (X,Y).(\mathsf{X},\mathsf{Y}).(X,Y).

We can prove this theorem using the unitary equivalence of purifications — but it isn't completely straightforward and we'll make use of a trick along the way.

To begin, consider spectral decompositions of the two density matrices ρ\rhoρ and σ.\sigma.σ.

ρ=∑a=0n−1pa∣ua⟩⟨ua∣σ=∑b=0n−1qb∣vb⟩⟨vb∣\begin{aligned} \rho & = \sum_{a = 0}^{n-1} p_a \vert u_a\rangle\langle u_a\vert \\[2mm] \sigma & = \sum_{b = 0}^{n-1} q_b \vert v_b\rangle\langle v_b\vert \end{aligned}ρσ​=a=0∑n−1​pa​∣ua​⟩⟨ua​∣=b=0∑n−1​qb​∣vb​⟩⟨vb​∣​

The two collections {∣u0⟩,…,∣un−1⟩}\{\vert u_0 \rangle,\ldots,\vert u_{n-1}\rangle\}{∣u0​⟩,…,∣un−1​⟩} and {∣v0⟩,…,∣vn−1⟩}\{\vert v_0 \rangle,\ldots,\vert v_{n-1}\rangle\}{∣v0​⟩,…,∣vn−1​⟩} are orthonormal bases of eigenvectors of ρ\rhoρ and σ,\sigma,σ, respectively, and p0,…,pn−1p_0,\ldots,p_{n-1}p0​,…,pn−1​ and q0,…,qn−1q_0,\ldots,q_{n-1}q0​,…,qn−1​ are the corresponding eigenvalues.

⟨c∣w‾⟩=⟨c∣w⟩‾\langle c \vert \overline{w}\rangle = \overline{\langle c \vert w\rangle}⟨c∣w⟩=⟨c∣w⟩​

Now consider the following two vectors ∣ϕ⟩\vert\phi\rangle∣ϕ⟩ and ∣ψ⟩,\vert\psi\rangle,∣ψ⟩, which are purifications of ρ\rhoρ and σ,\sigma,σ, respectively.

∣ϕ⟩=∑a=0n−1pa ∣ua⟩⊗∣ua‾⟩∣ψ⟩=∑b=0n−1qb ∣vb⟩⊗∣vb‾⟩\begin{aligned} \vert\phi\rangle & = \sum_{a = 0}^{n-1} \sqrt{p_a}\, \vert u_a\rangle \otimes \vert \overline{u_a}\rangle \\[2mm] \vert\psi\rangle & = \sum_{b = 0}^{n-1} \sqrt{q_b}\, \vert v_b\rangle \otimes \vert \overline{v_b}\rangle \end{aligned}∣ϕ⟩∣ψ⟩​=a=0∑n−1​pa​​∣ua​⟩⊗∣ua​​⟩=b=0∑n−1​qb​​∣vb​⟩⊗∣vb​​⟩​
