Naimark's theorem

Hindi pa naisalin

Ang pahinang ito ay hindi pa naisalin. Nakikita mo ang orihinal na bersyon sa Ingles.

Naimark's theorem is a fundamental fact concerning measurements. It states that every general measurement can be implemented in a simple way that's reminiscent of Stinespring representations of channels:

  1. The system to be measured is first combined with an initialized workspace system, forming a compound system.
  2. A unitary operation is then performed on the compound system.
  3. Finally, the workspace system is measured with respect to a standard basis measurement, yielding the outcome of the original general measurement.

Theorem statement and proof​

Let X\mathsf{X}X be a system and let {P0,…,Pm−1}\{P_0,\ldots,P_{m-1}\}{P0​,…,Pm−1​} be a collection of positive semidefinite matrices satisfying

P0+⋯+Pm−1=IX,P_0 + \cdots + P_{m-1} = \mathbb{I}_{\mathsf{X}},P0​+⋯+Pm−1​=IX​,

which is to say that they describe a measurement of X.\mathsf{X}.X. Also let Y\mathsf{Y}Y be a system whose classical state set is {0,…,m−1},\{0,\ldots,m-1\},{0,…,m−1}, which is the set of possible outcomes of this measurement.

Naimark's theorem states that there exists a unitary operation UUU on the compound system (Y,X)(\mathsf{Y},\mathsf{X})(Y,X) so that the implementation suggested by the following figure yields measurement outcomes that agree with the given measurement {P0,…,Pm−1},\{P_0,\ldots,P_{m-1}\},{P0​,…,Pm−1​}, meaning that the probabilities for the different possible measurement outcomes are precisely in agreement.

To be clear, the system X\mathsf{X}X starts out in some arbitrary state ρ\rhoρ while Y\mathsf{Y}Y is initialized to the ∣0⟩\vert 0\rangle∣0⟩ state. The unitary operation UUU is applied to (Y,X)(\mathsf{Y},\mathsf{X})(Y,X) and then the system Y\mathsf{Y}Y is measured with a standard basis measurement, yielding some outcome a∈{0,…,m−1}.a\in\{0,\ldots,m-1\}.a∈{0,…,m−1}.

The system X\mathsf{X}X is pictured as part of the output of the circuit, but for now we won't concern ourselves with the state of X\mathsf{X}X after UUU is performed, and can imagine that it is traced out. We'll be interested in the state of X\mathsf{X}X after UUU is performed later in the lesson, though.

An implementation of a measurement in this way is clearly reminiscent of a Stinespring representation of a channel, and the mathematical underpinnings are similar as well. The difference here is that the workspace system is measured rather than being traced out like in the case of a Stinespring representation.

The fact that every measurement can be implemented in this way is pretty simple to prove, but we're going to need a fact concerning positive semidefinite matrices first.

Fact

Suppose PPP is an n×nn \times nn×n positive semidefinite matrix. There exists a unique n×nn\times nn×n positive semidefinite matrix QQQ for which Q2=P.Q^2 = P.Q2=P. This unique positive semidefinite matrix is called the square root of PPP and is denoted P.\sqrt{P}.P​.

One way to find the square root of a positive semidefinite matrix is to first compute a spectral decomposition.

P=∑k=0n−1λk∣ψk⟩⟨ψk∣P = \sum_{k=0}^{n-1} \lambda_k \vert \psi_k \rangle \langle \psi_k \vertP=k=0∑n−1​λk​∣ψk​⟩⟨ψk​∣

Because PPP is positive semidefinite, its eigenvalues must be nonnegative real numbers, and by replacing them with their square roots we obtain an expression for the square root of P.P.P.

P=∑k=0n−1λk∣ψk⟩⟨ψk∣\sqrt{P} = \sum_{k=0}^{n-1} \sqrt{\lambda_k} \vert \psi_k \rangle \langle \psi_k \vertP​=k=0∑n−1​λk​​∣ψk​⟩⟨ψk​∣

With this concept in hand, we're ready to prove Naimark's theorem. Under the assumption that X\mathsf{X}X has nnn classical states, a unitary operation UUU on the pair (Y,X)(\mathsf{Y},\mathsf{X})(Y,X) can be represented by an nm×nmnm\times nmnm×nm matrix, which we can view as an m×mm\times mm×m block matrix whose blocks are n×n.n\times n.n×n. U=(P0?⋯?P1?⋯?⋮⋮⋱⋮Pm−1?⋯?)U = \begin{pmatrix} \sqrt{P_0} & \fbox{?} & \cdots & \fbox{?} \\[1mm] \sqrt{P_1} & \fbox{?} & \cdots & \fbox{?} \\[1mm] \vdots & \vdots & \ddots & \vdots\\[1mm] \sqrt{P_{m-1}} & \fbox{?} & \cdots & \fbox{?} \end{pmatrix}U=​P0​​P1​​⋮Pm−1​​​?​?​⋮?​​⋯⋯⋱⋯​?​?​⋮?​​​

It remains to check that the measurement outcome probabilities for the simulation are consistent with the original measurement. For a given initial state ρ\rhoρ of X,\mathsf{X},X, the measurement described by the collection {P0,…,Pm−1}\{P_0,\ldots,P_{m-1}\}{P0​,…,Pm−1​} results in each outcome a∈{0,…,m−1}a\in\{0,\ldots,m-1\}a∈{0,…,m−1} with probability Tr⁡(Paρ).\operatorname{Tr}(P_a \rho).Tr(Pa​ρ).

Non-destructive measurements​

So far in the lesson, we've concerned ourselves with destructive measurements, where the output consists of the classical measurement result alone and there is no specification of the post-measurement quantum state of the system that was measured.

Non-destructive measurements, on the other hand, do precisely this. Specifically, non-destructive measurements describe not only the classical measurement outcome probabilities, but also the state of the system that was measured conditioned on each possible measurement outcome. Note that the term non-destructive refers to the system being measured but not necessarily its state, which could change significantly as a result of the measurement.

In general, for a given destructive measurement, there will be multiple (in fact infinitely many) non-destructive measurements that are compatible with the given destructive measurement, meaning that the classical measurement outcome probabilities match precisely with the destructive measurement. So, there isn't a unique way to define the post-measurement quantum state of a system for a given measurement.

It is, in fact, possible to generalize non-destructive measurements even further, so that they produce a classical measurement outcome along with a quantum state output of a system that isn't necessarily the same as the input system.

The notion of a non-destructive measurement is an interesting and useful abstraction. It should, however, be recognized that non-destructive measurements can always be described as compositions of channels and destructive measurements — so there is a sense in which the notion of a destructive measurement is the more fundamental one.

From Naimark's theorem​

Consider the simulation of a general measurement like we have in Naimark's theorem. A simple way to obtain a non-destructive measurement from this simulation is revealed by the figure from before, where the system X\mathsf{X}X is not traced out, but is part of the output. This yields both a classical measurement outcome a∈{0,…,m−1}a\in\{0,\ldots,m-1\}a∈{0,…,m−1} as well as a post-measurement quantum state of X.\mathsf{X}.X.

Let's describe these states in mathematical terms. We're assuming that the initial state of X\mathsf{X}X is ρ,\rho,ρ, so that after the initialized system Y\mathsf{Y}Y is introduced and UUU is performed, we have that (Y,X)(\mathsf{Y},\mathsf{X})(Y,X) is in the state

The probabilities for the different classical outcomes to appear are the same as before — they can't change as a result of us deciding to ignore or not ignore X.\mathsf{X}.X. That is, we obtain each a∈{0,…,m−1}a\in\{0,\ldots,m-1\}a∈{0,…,m−1} with probability Tr⁡(Paρ).\operatorname{Tr}(P_a \rho).Tr(Pa​ρ).

From a Kraus representation​

There are alternative selections for UUU in the context of Naimark's theorem that produce the same measurement outcome probabilities but give entirely different output states of X.\mathsf{X}.X.

For instance, one option is to substitute (IY⊗V)U(\mathbb{I}_{\mathsf{Y}} \otimes V) U(IY​⊗V)U for U,U,U, where VVV is any unitary operation on X.\mathsf{X}.X. Generalizations​

There are even more general ways to formulate non-destructive measurements than the ways we've discussed. The notion of a quantum instrument (which won't be described here) represents one way to do this.
